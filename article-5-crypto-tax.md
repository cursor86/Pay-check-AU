# Crypto Tax in Australia 2026: What Every Investor Needs to Know

*Published by SS Traders | May 2026 | 8 min read*

---

The ATO is watching. Since 2019, the Australian Taxation Office has been actively data-matching crypto transactions with Australian exchanges — and the number of compliance letters and audits has grown every year since. If you've bought, sold, swapped, or spent cryptocurrency in Australia and haven't been reporting it correctly, this guide could save you from a very unpleasant tax surprise.

Here's everything Australian crypto investors need to know for the 2025–26 financial year.

---

## Is Crypto Taxed in Australia?

Yes — unambiguously. The ATO treats cryptocurrency as an asset (property), not currency. This means that almost every time you dispose of crypto, it's a taxable event subject to Capital Gains Tax (CGT).

The ATO's position has been clear since 2014 and has only become more strictly enforced since then. Ignorance is not an accepted defence, and "I didn't know I had to report it" will not reduce your tax liability or penalties.

---

## What Is a Taxable Event?

A taxable event occurs when you **dispose** of cryptocurrency. This includes:

- **Selling crypto for AUD** (most obvious one)
- **Swapping one crypto for another** (e.g. Bitcoin to Ethereum — this is a disposal of Bitcoin)
- **Spending crypto to buy goods or services**
- **Receiving payment in crypto** (as income, at market value at time of receipt)
- **DeFi activities** including providing liquidity, earning yield, staking rewards (in most cases)
- **NFT transactions** — buying, selling, or minting NFTs

What is generally **not** a taxable event:
- Buying crypto with AUD (you're acquiring, not disposing)
- Transferring crypto between your own wallets
- HODLing (simply holding crypto that increases in value — no tax until you sell).

## How Is the Tax Calculated?

### Capital Gains Tax (CGT)

Your capital gain (or loss) is the difference between what you received for the crypto and what you paid for it (your "cost base").

**Capital Gain = Sale Price – Cost Base**

The cost base includes the purchase price plus any fees you paid to acquire it (e.g. exchange fees, gas fees).

This gain is then added to your taxable income for the financial year and taxed at your marginal income tax rate.

### The 12-Month CGT Discount

Here's the most important tax planning tool for crypto investors: if you hold a crypto asset for **more than 12 months** before selling, you're entitled to a **50% CGT discount** on your capital gain.

**Example:**
- You buy $5,000 of Bitcoin in January 2025
- You sell in March 2026 (14 months later) for $9,000
- Capital gain = $4,000
- With 50% discount: only $2,000 is added to your taxable income
- If your marginal rate is 32.5%, you pay $650 in tax instead of $1,300

This is why long-term "Buy and Hold" is not just an investment strategy — it's a tax strategy.

### Capital Losses

If you sell crypto for less than you paid, you have a capital loss. Capital losses can be used to **offset capital gains** in the same or future financial years — but they cannot be used to offset regular income (like your salary).

If you have more capital losses than gains in a given year, the excess carries forward indefinitely until you have capital gains to offset.

## Crypto as Income

Not all crypto is treated as a capital gain. In some cases, crypto you receive is treated as **ordinary income** — taxed at your marginal rate with no CGT discount available:

- **Mining rewards:** If you mine cryptocurrency, the fair market value at the time you receive it is income
- **Staking rewards:** Generally treated as income when received (the ATO's position on this has evolved — consult a crypto tax specialist for complex staking situations)
- **Airdrops:** May be income if you performed a service to receive them
- **Salary or payment in crypto:** Taxed as income at market value on the day received

The cost base of any crypto treated as income is the value at the time it was received. When you eventually sell, any further gain is then calculated as a capital gain from that point.

## How Does the ATO Know About My Crypto?

The ATO has formal data-sharing arrangements with Australian crypto exchanges including CoinSpot, Swyftx, Binance AU, and others. This means the ATO can see:

- Your identity (KYC information you provided when signing up)
- The amounts deposited and withdrawn
- Trading history in many cases

The ATO also uses blockchain analytics tools to trace transactions beyond just exchange data. Assuming your crypto activity is invisible is an extremely risky position to take.

---

## Record Keeping: What You Need

The ATO requires you to keep records of every crypto transaction, including:

- The date of each transaction
- The value in AUD at the time of the transaction
- What was received and what was given up
- Exchange or wallet fees
- Bank records and exchange statements

Most Australian exchanges provide a downloadable transaction history — export this at the end of each financial year and store it securely. You're required to keep records for at least 5 years after lodging your return.

## Common Mistakes Australian Crypto Investors Make

**1. Not reporting crypto-to-crypto swaps**
Every swap is a disposal. If you swapped ETH for SOL, you need to report the capital gain or loss on the ETH at the time of the swap. This catches many investors by surprise.

**2. Losing track of cost bases**
If you can't prove what you paid for your crypto, the ATO may assume your cost base is zero — meaning your entire sale price is a capital gain. Keep records.

**3. Forgetting DeFi and staking**
The DeFi space creates complex tax events. Providing liquidity, receiving LP tokens, earning yield — all may have tax implications. Seek specialist advice if you're active in DeFi.

**4. Assuming losses don't need to be reported**
Capital losses must be reported even if you'd rather forget about them — they can offset future gains and reduce your tax bill in future years.

**5. Waiting until tax time**
The end of financial year is too late to make strategic decisions. Planning throughout the year — such as deciding whether to hold for the 12-month discount or realise a loss to offset a gain — can significantly reduce your tax.

## Key Takeaways

- The ATO taxes crypto as property — CGT applies to almost every disposal

- The ATO receives data from Australian exchanges — don't assume privacy

- Capital losses can offset capital gains now and in future years
