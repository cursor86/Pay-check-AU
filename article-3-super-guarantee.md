# Super Guarantee 2025–26: What Your Employer Owes You (And How to Check)

*Published by SS Traders | May 2026 | 7 min read*

---

Most Australians know they have superannuation, but surprisingly few understand exactly how much their employer is required to pay, when it must be paid, and how to check they're actually receiving it. In 2025–26, the Super Guarantee rate is 12% — and knowing your rights could be worth tens of thousands of dollars over your career.

---

## What Is the Super Guarantee?

The Superannuation Guarantee (SG) is a compulsory system that requires Australian employers to make regular contributions to their employees' superannuation funds. It's designed to help Australians save for retirement without having to think about it — money is set aside automatically every time you're paid.

The SG is calculated as a percentage of your Ordinary Time Earnings (OTE) — roughly, your regular salary before overtime.

---

## What's the Super Guarantee Rate in 2025–26?

The Super Guarantee rate for the 2025–26 financial year is **12%**.

This is the result of a legislated schedule of increases that has been gradually lifting the SG rate from 9.5% in 2021 toward 12% — which was reached in 2025–26

### SG Rate History:
| Financial Year | SG Rate |
|---|---|
| 2021–22 | 10.0% |
| 2022–23 | 10.5% |
| 2023–24 | 11.0% |
| 2024–25 | 11.5% |
| 2025–26 | 12.0% |

---

## How Is Super Calculated?

Your super is calculated on your Ordinary Time Earnings — which includes your base salary, commissions, certain allowances, and some bonuses. It generally does not include overtime pay.

### Example:

If your base salary is $85,000:
- Super Guarantee (12%) = $10,200/year
- This is paid on top of your salary — it doesn't come out of your take-home pay
- Your employer's total cost of employing you = $95,200

This is an important distinction: super is an *additional* cost to your employer, not a deduction from your salary (unless your contract states otherwise — some packages are quoted as "total remuneration" inclusive of super).

---

## Is Super Included in My Salary Package?

This depends on how your employment contract is structured:

**Salary + Super:** Your contract shows $85,000 base salary, and your employer pays 12% super on top. Total employer cost = $95,200.

**Total Remuneration Package:** Your contract shows $95,200 "total package inclusive of super." In this case, your base salary is roughly $85,000 and your super comes out of the $95,200 — you're not getting extra.

Always clarify this when starting a new job or negotiating a salary. If a job ad says "$95,000 package," ask whether that's inclusive or exclusive of super.

---

## When Must Employers Pay Super?

Your employer is required to pay your super contributions at least **quarterly** — within 28 days after the end of each quarter:

| Quarter | Payment Deadline |
|---|---|
| 1 July – 30 September | 28 October |
| 1 October – 31 December | 28 January |
| 1 January – 31 March | 28 April |
| 1 April – 30 June | 28 July |

However, many employers pay super more frequently — fortnightly or monthly — in line with pay cycles. The ATO's Payday Super rules, which take effect from 1 July 2026, will require super to be paid on the same day as wages for most employers.

## How to Check You're Receiving Your Super

Too many Australians discover years later that their employer wasn't paying their super. Here's how to check:

### 1. Check your super fund account
Log into your super fund's online portal or app. You should see regular contributions appearing. If they're not showing up quarterly at minimum, something is wrong.

### 2. Check your payslip
Your payslip should show the super amount being allocated. Note: the payslip shows what your employer has *allocated* — not necessarily what has been *paid* to the fund. Always cross-check with your actual fund balance.

### 3. Use myGov / ATO Online
Log into myGov, link your ATO account, and check "Super" in the ATO online services. You can see all super funds in your name and recent contributions reported by employers.

### 4. Contact the ATO
If you suspect your employer hasn't been paying, you can report it to the ATO confidentially at ato.gov.au. The ATO can pursue unpaid super on your behalf.

## What Is the Super Guarantee Charge?

If an employer fails to pay the correct super by the due date, they're liable for the **Super Guarantee Charge (SGC)** — which includes:

- The unpaid super amount
- Interest of 10% per annum
- An administration fee

The SGC is not tax-deductible for employers — making it significantly more expensive than simply paying on time. This is meant to incentivise compliance.

---

## Can You Make Extra Super Contributions?

Yes — and it's one of the smartest things you can do for your financial future. There are two main types of voluntary contributions:

**Concessional (before-tax) contributions:** These include your employer's SG payments plus any salary sacrifice or personal contributions you claim a tax deduction for. The concessional cap for 2025–26 is $30,000/year. Contributions are taxed at 15% inside super — much lower than most people's marginal tax rate.

**Non-concessional (after-tax) contributions:** These are contributions from your take-home pay that you don't claim a deduction for. The cap is $120,000/year (or up to $360,000 over three years using the bring-forward rule). These grow tax-free inside super.

## Key Takeaways

- The Super Guarantee rate is 12% in 2025–26 — on top of your salary
- Check whether your package is "inclusive" or "exclusive" of super when negotiating
- Employers must pay super at least quarterly — from July 2026, it must be paid on payday
- Check your super fund regularly to make sure contributions are arriving
- You can report unpaid super to the ATO for free
- Voluntary contributions can significantly boost your retirement savings
