# What Is the Medicare Levy and Do You Have to Pay It?

*Published by SS Traders | May 2026 | 6 min read*

---

Every year, millions of Australians pay the Medicare Levy without really understanding what it is, who pays it,or whether they can reduce or avoid it. If you've noticed a 2% deduction on your payslip or tax return and wondered what it's for — this guide is for you.

---

## What Is the Medicare Levy?

The Medicare Levy is a charge applied to your taxable income to help fund Australia's public health system, Medicare. Medicare gives Australians access to subsidised medical services, free treatment in public hospitals, and cheaper prescription medicines through the Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme (PBS).

For most Australians, the Medicare Levy is set at **2% of your taxable income** for the 2025–26 financial year.

### Example:
- Salary of $70,000 → Medicare Levy = $1,400/year
- Salary of $100,000 → Medicare Levy = $2,000/year
- Salary of $130,000 → Medicare Levy = $2,600/year

It's calculated on top of your regular income tax — so your total tax bill is income tax + Medicare Levy.

---

## Who Has to Pay the Medicare Levy?

Most Australian residents who lodge a tax return pay the full 2% levy. However, there are some exceptions.

### You may be exempt if:
- You are a foreign resident for tax purposes
- You hold a certain visa that doesn't grant access to Medicare (e.g. some temporary visas)
- Your income is below the low-income threshold

### Low-income thresholds for 2025–26:

| Category | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Individuals | Below ~$26,000 |
| Families | Below ~$43,800 (plus ~$4,000 per dependent child) |
| Seniors and pensioners | Higher thresholds apply |

If your income falls below these thresholds, you either pay no levy or a reduced amount. The ATO calculates this automatically when you lodge your tax return.

---

## What Is the Medicare Levy Surcharge?

This is separate from the standard Medicare Levy and often confuses people. The Medicare Levy Surcharge (MLS) is an *additional* charge on top of the standard 2% — and it only applies if:

1. You earn above $93,000 as a single (or $186,000 as a family) for 2025–26, AND
2. You do **not** hold an approved private hospital health insurance policy.

The surcharge rates are:

| Income | Surcharge Rate |
|---|---|
| $93,001 – $108,000 | 1.0% |
| $108,001 – $144,000 | 1.25% |
| $144,001 and above | 1.5% |

### The key takeaway:
If you earn over $93,000 and don't have private hospital cover, you'll pay up to 1.5% extra on top of your regular 2% Medicare Levy — meaning you could be paying 3.5% of your income in health-related levies alone.

For many higher earners, taking out basic private hospital cover (which can cost as little as $1,200–$1,800/year) actually saves money compared to paying the surcharge.

---

## Does Private Health Insurance Reduce the Levy?

Having private health insurance does **not** reduce the standard 2% Medicare Levy — everyone pays that regardless. However, having an approved hospital cover policy means you avoid the Medicare Levy Surcharge if you're a higher earner.

Additionally, if you're 31 or older and don't have private hospital cover, you may be subject to the Lifetime Health Cover loading — an extra 2% added to your premiums for every year you delay taking out cover after age 30.

---

## How Is the Medicare Levy Calculated and Collected?

For employees, the Medicare Levy is typically withheld from your pay throughout the year as part of your PAYG (Pay As You Go) withholding — the same process that collects your income tax. Your employer estimates your annual income and withholds the appropriate amount each pay period.

When you lodge your annual tax return, the ATO reconciles the total amount withheld against your actual income for the year. If too much was withheld, you get a refund. If too little, you pay the difference.

---

## Medicare Levy vs Medicare: What's the Difference?

The Medicare Levy is the *tax* you pay to fund Medicare. Medicare itself is the government health insurance scheme that gives you access to:

- Bulk-billed doctor visits (when your GP bulk bills)
- Free or subsidised hospital treatment in public hospitals
- Subsidised medicines via the PBS
- Specialist and allied health rebates

The levy collected doesn't necessarily go into a ring-fenced fund just for Medicare — it goes into consolidated government revenue, and the government then funds Medicare from that.

---

## Can You Claim the Medicare Levy Back?

You can't "claim back" the levy in the way you'd claim a tax deduction. However, you may be entitled to a Medicare Levy reduction or exemption if:

- Your income is below the low-income threshold
- You were not entitled to Medicare for part of the year (e.g. on a certain visa)
- You are a senior or pensioner with lower income

These are applied automatically when you lodge your tax return through myTax or via a registered tax agent.

## Key Takeaways

- The Medicare Levy is 2% of your taxable income, paid by most Australian residents
- Low-income earners may be exempt or pay a reduced amount
- High-income earners without private hospital cover pay an additional Medicare Levy Surcharge of 1–1.5%
- The levy is withheld from your pay throughout the year — reconciled at tax time
- Private hospital cover can save high earners money by avoiding the surcharge
